A
Version A

AI with Purpose
and Conscience

"We're not just learning to use AI. We're deciding what kind of leaders we want to be in the age of AI."
Full Day · 7 hrs 15–50 Participants In-Person Leadership Teams English or Danish
01 / 10
Background

We are living through the fastest technological shift in human history. AI is no longer a future scenario — it is reshaping how organisations work, how decisions get made, and what skills matter, right now.

Most leaders are being asked to act before they understand. And those building AI tools are moving fast — with little time or incentive to ask whether they should. The Center for Humane Technology calls this the "wisdom gap": the distance between the speed of technology and our capacity to respond wisely.

Bigger Picture has spent 30 years helping organisations think together about complex challenges. This workshop brings that same rigour to the most consequential question of our time: how do we lead responsibly in the age of AI?

02 / 10
Purpose

To give leadership teams a shared foundation for understanding AI — its capabilities, its limits, its risks, and its potential — and to equip them to make decisions that serve not just their organisation, but the people and communities it affects.

"AI is nuclear-level technology. We need everyone at the table to understand it — not just the engineers."
03 / 10
Participants

Leadership teams, senior managers, and business unit owners who carry strategic responsibility. People who sense something bigger is at stake and want to engage seriously — not just get a briefing.

  • 15–50 participants per session
  • Mixed roles welcome — the conversation is richer with diversity
  • No technical background required
  • A technical co-facilitator joins from outside Bigger Picture to handle live demos and nuts and bolts
04 / 10
Participant Experience
Before — they arrive thinking
  • "Everyone is talking about AI but nobody agrees on what to do."
  • "I feel behind and don't want to show it."
  • "I worry we're missing something important — but also that we'll do something harmful."
  • "Is this hype, or is this real?"
After — they leave saying
  • "I finally have a picture I can work with."
  • "I know what questions I need to ask — and who to ask them with."
  • "We have a shared language now."
  • "I'm not just thinking about efficiency. I'm thinking about responsibility."
05 / 10
Objectives
  • Build a shared, honest picture of AI — capabilities, limits, risks, and opportunity
  • Surface the ethical and moral questions that need to be part of every AI decision
  • Explore real dilemmas through structured visual tools
  • Give each participant a personal leadership roadmap for the next 90 days
  • Leave with tools and language to continue the conversation inside their organisation
06 / 10
Results — What's on the Wall
  • A large visual AI landscape map — co-created by participants, showing the terrain they're navigating
  • A dilemma wall — real tensions named, explored, and partially resolved
  • A "What Matters Here" canvas — the problems in their context that AI could help with, and what to avoid
  • Individual personal roadmaps on paper — what to learn, what to start, what to protect
  • A shared Miro board participants can take back into their teams
07 / 10
Situation — The Room

A large, open workshop space. Tables of 4–5 people with ample wall space. Each table has markers, sticky notes, and A3 paper for sketching. Each participant has a laptop or tablet available for live exploration.

  • Long paper rolls on the walls for group mapping (70cm × 2.5m per table)
  • Multiple projectors — one for facilitation, one for live AI demos
  • Miro boards pre-loaded and accessible on all devices
  • One technical co-facilitator available throughout — not leading, but present
  • 15–50 participants; ideal sweet spot is 20–30

10 / 10
Guiding Principles
No wrong questions

Leaders are encouraged to name what they don't know. That's the starting point, not the weakness.

Visible thinking

Everything gets drawn, mapped, or written on the wall. Nothing stays in people's heads.

Ethical responsibility

We approach AI as a shared societal challenge, not just an organisational efficiency question.

Generous participation

All experience levels are valued. The engineer and the sceptic both belong in this room.

B
Version B

AI at Work —
A Hands-On Leader's Day

"Stop watching from the sidelines. Build something. See what's possible. Then decide what's right."
Full Day · 7 hrs 15–50 Participants In-Person Leadership Teams English or Danish
01 / 10
Background

AI tools are already in use across most organisations — by some people, in some teams, in fragments. The challenge is not awareness. It's coherence. Leaders are making decisions about AI without shared understanding, common frameworks, or hands-on experience.

The result is a patchwork: some experiments moving fast, others stuck in approval loops, and a leadership team that can't have a real conversation because they're not working from the same picture.

This workshop closes that gap — through doing, not just discussing.

02 / 10
Purpose

To give leadership teams hands-on experience with AI tools, a shared visual framework for how AI works in an organisational context, and a prototype of their own AI workflow — built, tested, and refined on the day.

"Context is everything. Until you've built your first AI operating structure, you're just guessing."
03 / 10
Participants

Leaders and managers who are pragmatic, action-oriented, and ready to get their hands dirty. They may be sceptical or enthusiastic — both are welcome. They want to understand by doing, not by being briefed.

  • 15–50 participants; tables of 4–5
  • Each table ideally has at least one person who has used AI tools before
  • Participants bring a real use case or workflow challenge from their own work
  • Technical co-facilitator present throughout for live support
04 / 10
Participant Experience
Before — they arrive thinking
  • "I've tried ChatGPT a few times but I don't really get it."
  • "My team is using AI but I can't evaluate what they're doing."
  • "I know we need to do something. I just don't know where to start."
  • "I don't want to be the last one to understand this."
After — they leave saying
  • "I built something today. I know how this works now."
  • "I understand what context means — and why it changes everything."
  • "I have a roadmap. I know the next three things I need to do."
  • "And I know what questions to keep asking as we go."
05 / 10
Objectives
  • Demystify AI — move from abstract to tangible through direct experience
  • Understand the architecture of effective AI use: context, structure, memory, and workflow
  • Design and sketch a first AI operating model relevant to their own context
  • Test it live — observe what works and what needs rethinking
  • Surface the bigger questions that emerge from practice — ethics, workforce, purpose
06 / 10
Results — What's on the Wall
  • A large visual AI workflow map per table — hand-drawn on paper, showing their context, structure, and key decision points
  • A live-built AI project skeleton from the fishbowl case — shown, tested, and discussed
  • A "questions we need to answer" wall — the bigger questions that emerged through doing
  • Individual roadmaps: first workflow to build, first conversation to have, first thing to read
  • Miro board with all templates to take back into their teams
07 / 10
Situation — The Room

Workshop space set for active making — not passive receiving. Tables of 4–5 with large paper rolls on the walls and every surface available for working. Laptops or tablets on every table.

  • Long paper rolls (70cm × 2.5m) for each table's AI workflow map
  • Markers, sticky notes, structured template cards at each table
  • Two projectors: one for facilitation, one always live with an AI tool
  • Miro boards pre-loaded with workflow design templates
  • Technical co-facilitator moves between tables during building phases
08 / 10 — Process Steps · Full Day Arc · Version B
09:00–09:45
Welcome & Ground Zero
Where are we right now with AI? Quick temperature check across the room. The landscape in 10 minutes — no hype, no fear. Then: here's what we'll build today.
Plenary · Quick Input
09:45–11:00
How AI Actually Works
Not theory — demonstration. Live: what context means, how structure changes everything, why prompting alone is not enough. Technical co-facilitator shows it in real time.
Demo + Q&A
11:00–12:00
Map Your Own Terrain
Each table maps their own AI situation: what they use now, what they need, what's missing. Long paper on the wall. Structured template guides the stops they need to make.
Table Work · Visual
12:00–13:00
Lunch
Gallery walk encouraged. Each table explains their map to others. Informal exchange. Energy stays high.
Break · Gallery Walk
13:00–14:30
Design Your First Workflow
Using the visual template, each table drafts their first AI operating structure: purpose, tone, memory, tasks, review points. This is the first version of their own Claude project skeleton.
Deep Design Work
14:30–15:45
Fishbowl: Build It Live
One table's workflow built live in front of the room. Everyone watches, questions, improves. The bigger questions surface naturally: what about our people? What are we optimising for?
Fishbowl · Live Build
15:45–17:00
Roadmap + The Big Questions
Personal roadmap: what to build first, who to involve, what to ask. Then: what questions emerged today that we need to keep asking? Closing reflection and commitments.
Individual + Plenary
09 / 10
Next Steps
  • Each participant leaves with their hand-drawn workflow map and a digital Miro copy
  • Bigger Picture shares a "Start Here" kit within 48 hours — practical templates, reading list, a guide to building your first Claude project
  • Optional: 90-day follow-up half-day where tables bring back what they built and what they learned
  • Optional: Vico AI assistant pre-configured for their first workflow
  • All wall outputs photographed and compiled into a visual summary document
10 / 10
Guiding Principles
Learning by doing

Nobody learns to swim by watching. We build something today, even if it is imperfect.

Visible thinking

Every decision, every structure, every question goes on the wall. The map is the output.

Questions over answers

The best thing to leave with is better questions — not a finished product.

Purposeful momentum

Speed matters, but not more than direction. We move fast and ask why at every step.
